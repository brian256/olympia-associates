Who Answers for This Decision?

Dan Davies calls it an accountability sink — a structure built so that consequences get absorbed before they can travel back to the people who caused them. This is not incompetence. It is architecture.

Read the full article

Originally published February 2026 · LinkedIn

From the ML researchers who built LaundryML — a tool that generates clean-looking explanations for biased AI models — to Uganda's 2026 elections, to Alberta's emergency rooms, to the aftermath of a cartel operation in Puerto Vallarta: the same architecture appears across different countries and different systems.

The feedback loop between those who bear the consequences and those who made the decisions is not slow. It is severed, intentionally. Accountability sinks are not new. What AI provides is an escalated and expanded version of something we already knew how to do.

The solution is not a better algorithm. It is not a stronger resolution. It is leaders — in technology, in government, in business, in community — who are willing to remain connected to the consequences of their decisions.

Transformative Communication: Why Most Teams Talk Past Each Other

When leaders were asked what percentage of their problems stem from poor communication, the lowest answer was sixty percent.

Most organisations treat communication as a soft skill — a vague aspiration that gets a slot in the annual appraisal cycle and a training module nobody remembers. The gap between how leaders believe they communicate and how their teams experience them is consistently wider than any of them expect. An open letter to Dr. Lawrence Muganga on Uganda's AI future — a practitioner's rigorous engagement with a $1.2 billion national AI proposal.

Uganda's nominal GDP stood at $56.6 billion in 2024. The $500 billion target Dr. Muganga's proposal requires sustained annual growth of 14–18% for 14 consecutive years — a rate no sub-Saharan African economy has achieved at scale. This article runs the feasibility check, costs a credible alternative, and makes the case that the wrong mechanism does not merely delay the outcome — it consumes the capital and political will needed to build the right one.

Burnout Is Not a Personal Problem. It's a Leadership Signal.

When a leader's team burns out while the leader appears fine, that is a different and more serious signal.

The most important thing to understand about burnout in teams is this: leaders model the relationship with work that their teams replicate. A leader who never disconnects communicates — regardless of what they say in team meetings — that disconnection is not actually acceptable. This article covers the neurochemistry, the four diagnostic questions, and what recovery actually requires structurally rather than tactically.

Values-Driven Leadership: Why Ethical Leaders Win in the Long Run

Being a values-driven leader is often inconvenient, occasionally costly, and always worth it.

In a world obsessed with quarterly results, ethical leadership can feel like swimming upstream. But organisations whose leaders hold to their values consistently outperform, outlast, and outshine their competition — not because virtue is rewarded in some abstract sense, but because trust is a structural asset. This article examines the three dimensions that actually matter, why values without systems are decoration, and the vulnerability question most leaders avoid asking.
