Burnout Is Not a Personal Problem. It's a Leadership Signal.

When a leader burns out, it is rarely just a personal problem. It is almost always a signal. And when a leader's team burns out while the leader appears fine, that is a different and more serious signal still.

Brian Ssennoga · Olympia & Associates May 2026

I've been burnt out. Properly, functionally burnt out — the kind where someone asks how you're doing and you have to pause before answering, not because you're thinking of an interesting response, but because you genuinely don't know.

I know what it looks like from the inside: seventeen browser tabs, two messaging apps, a report that was due yesterday, and the uncomfortable awareness that you've forgotten what it feels like to do something that has nothing to do with work. The hobbies disappeared so gradually you didn't notice until someone asked and you had nothing to say.

I share this because it is the honest starting point for what I want to say about burnout in organisations — and what I want to say is this: when a leader burns out, it is rarely just a personal problem. It is almost always a signal. And when a leader's team burns out while the leader appears fine, that is a different and more serious signal still.

The Neurochemistry Doesn't Lie

Simon Sinek's Leaders Eat Last gives us a useful frame. When teams feel protected — psychologically safe, fairly treated, clear on their direction — the dominant neurochemical experience is oxytocin. Trust. Connection. The capacity to take creative risks without catastrophic self-protection kicking in.

When teams are under sustained, unmanaged pressure — unclear priorities, leaders who consume rather than generate energy, cultures where pace is a virtue and rest is a weakness — cortisol dominates. Cortisol is the stress hormone. It is useful in short bursts. At sustained organisational levels, it produces exactly the symptoms most leaders associate with "the pace of the business": hypervigilance, defensiveness, tunnel vision, declining quality of judgment, and the slow erosion of the human capacity for generosity.

Teams that are burning out do not look like teams that are failing. They often look like teams that are working very hard. The difference is only visible in the second and third derivative effects — the attrition that accelerates quietly, the innovation that stops appearing, the psychological safety that used to feel like a given and now requires active effort to maintain.

What Leaders Model

The most important thing to understand about burnout in teams is this: leaders model the relationship with work that their teams replicate. A leader who never disconnects communicates — regardless of what they say in team meetings about work-life balance — that disconnection is not actually acceptable. A leader who sends emails at 11pm and mentions it casually in the morning meeting is setting a norm that most team members will feel implicitly obligated to meet.

This is not a moral failing. It is a modelling effect. Leaders do it without intending to. The solution is not to perform relaxation — it is to actually practice it, visibly, and talk about why it matters. Not as a productivity hack. As a genuine belief about what sustainable excellent work requires.

The Four Questions

In leadership coaching, I use four diagnostic questions when burnout — personal or team-level — is in the room:

What is the pace asking us to give up? Every sustained pace above a certain threshold requires sacrifice. The question is whether the sacrifice is visible and chosen, or invisible and compounding. Teams that are burning out have usually been giving things up — quality, care, creativity, personal time — without anyone naming what is being traded and whether the trade is worth it.

Who absorbs the pressure when it spikes? In healthy organisations, pressure is distributed and absorbed at multiple levels, including leadership. In organisations heading toward burnout, pressure consistently flows downward. If you map the stress distribution in your team and it looks like a funnel, you have a structural problem, not a resilience problem.

What does recovery look like here? Every high-performing system requires recovery cycles. An organisation with no recovery cycles is not performing at a high level. It is depleting. The question is not whether your team works hard. It is whether there is structural space to recover between hard periods.

What does the leader do when they need to recover? Not what the leader says about recovery. What they actually do. Teams take their cues from what they observe, not from what they are told. If the answer is "nothing visible" or "I don't really stop," the team has its answer about whether stopping is genuinely permitted.

The Signal Worth Hearing

Burnout, when it arrives, is a signal. Not of weakness. Not of poor time management. Not of insufficient commitment to the work. It is a signal that the system — personal or organisational — has been running beyond its sustainable capacity for longer than it can absorb.

The honest response to that signal is not to push through it. It is to ask what the system has been trying to say, and what it would take to build something that works over a longer horizon. That question — what would it take to build something that works over a longer horizon — is one of the most important questions in leadership. It rarely gets asked while the pace feels sustainable. It almost always gets asked in the aftermath of something breaking. Better to ask it before.
